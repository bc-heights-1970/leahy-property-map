Under Leahy, Boston College extended its presence in Ireland by purchasing property in the center of Dublin, solidifying BC’s position as one of the leading American universities in Ireland and furthering the relationship between BC and Ireland.


The property, 42-43 St. Stephen’s Green, was acquired in April 1999 to house the Irish Institute and BC Ireland. The exact sale price is unknown, though sources told an Irish newspaper in 1999 that the sum totaled over £4 million.


BC Ireland supports the BC community in Ireland, and the Irish Institute works with programs to utilize American resources in assisting Ireland.


"The opening of the Boston College Center in Ireland is a natural extension of our commitment to working with the Irish people in both Ireland and Northern Ireland," Leahy said.


The property became a center for students, staff, and alumni in Ireland, with many events and resources. BC Ireland hosts students studying abroad for classes during the school year and the summer, as well as a summer internship program.


It has also worked with the U.S. Embassy, Ireland’s Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade, and the Irish U.S. Alumni Association.

The path to BC’s costliest land acquisition during Leahy’s tenure—the $172.4 million, 66-acre Brighton Campus—came as a result of one of the most publicized and consequential crises in American Catholic history.


In early 2002, The Boston Globe published an investigation that revealed clergy sex abuse and the lengths to which church leaders went to cover it up, triggering international scrutiny and a wave of lawsuits.


In 2002, the Archdiocese of Boston agreed to pay $10 million to victims of a single priest, and in 2003 it paid an additional $85 million to around 550 victims—at the time the largest such settlement in U.S. Catholic Church history.


The archdiocese promised that no parish funds would be used to pay off the settlements. To raise the money, it would have to sell some of its land. And some of its most valuable land sat directly across Commonwealth Avenue from BC’s Lower Campus.


The Brighton property—home to the archdiocese's central administration since the late 1920s—had been built over decades into an expansive compound of seminary buildings, chancery offices, and the former residence of a cardinal and archbishop of Boston, who had resigned due to the scandal.


In 2004, BC agreed to purchase 43 acres of the Brighton headquarters for $99.4 million. Church officials specified that the money would go toward paying off $90 million in loans taken out to settle the abuse lawsuits.


“The cost will be significant, but we could not pass up the opportunity for more land, especially parcels so close to our campus,” Leahy told The Pilot, a Boston-based Catholic newspaper.


Leahy added that BC would raise money “very aggressively” from alumni to help pay for the property. University Spokesman Jack Dunn said alumni would understand the importance of their purchase.


"I think our alumni will be supportive because they realize the opportunity that this sale presents to Boston College," Dunn said. "And it will help the Archdiocese of Boston in its time of need."


BC purchased an additional 3.25 acres of adjoining land two years later for $8 million, and the remaining 18 acres for $65 million in 2007. By the time the final deed was signed, BC had acquired 66 acres from the Archdiocese of Boston, along with a number of buildings.


Over the following two decades, BC methodically filled in, renovated, and repurposed the archdiocese’s land and buildings.


In 2007, BC established its School of Theology and Ministry on a portion of Brighton Campus, bringing together its own Institute of Religious Education and Pastoral Ministry and the Weston Jesuit School of Theology to form its fifth school within the University.


In 2016, the former cardinal's mansion was remodeled and reopened as the new home of BC's McMullen Museum of Art, with nearly double its previous exhibition space.


On Brighton Campus’ northernmost end, BC built the Harrington Athletics Village, which opened in 2018 to give BC's baseball and softball programs new full-time homes.


And in 2025, BC began the construction of the Catholic Religious Archives, a two-story, 44,000-square-foot facility on Brighton Campus that will preserve records and other archival materials from Catholic religious orders.


Today, Brighton Campus’s 520,884 square feet of buildings are also home to a community of Jesuits and offices for major administrative offices, including University Advancement, Human Resources, and University Communications.

Months after the first Brighton purchase, Leahy turned his attention to another religious property 20 miles southwest.


In 2004, BC acquired St. Stephen's Priory—a 78.5-acre estate along the Charles River in Dover, Mass.—for approximately $16.5 million.


The estate, built in 1902, was originally a private family home, with gardens and paths created by the designers of Central Park.


The Dominican Fathers purchased the estate in 1951 and operated it as a religious retreat for over five decades before deciding to close the priory in 2004.


BC acquired it to meet the rising demand for spaces to host conferences and retreats.


In 2005, Jack Connors, BC ’63, and his wife, Eileen, BC ’66, donated $10 million to establish the property as the Connors Family Retreat and Conference Center.


“I am confident that The Connors Family Retreat and Conference Center will contribute significantly to the personal and professional growth of numerous Boston College students, faculty, staff, alumni, and friends,” Leahy said in an interview with The Chronicle.


The space is now used for retreats, weddings, conferences, meetings, and other events.


The property bordering the Charles River includes 78.5 acres of wooded land, 70,000 square feet of buildings, and gardens.

The story of 300 Hammond Pond Parkway begins, like Brighton, with another religious institution.


Congregation Mishkan Tefila—considered the oldest conservative synagogue in Greater Boston—had occupied its Chestnut Hill site since the mid-1950s, when it acquired the property from the state. But its congregation had seen a decline in membership, and the synagogue was ready to move to smaller quarters.


In August 2015, Boston College announced an agreement to purchase the 24-acre parcel at 300 Hammond Pond Parkway, 1.2 miles from its main campus and surrounded by conservation land.


The deal closed in 2016 for $20 million. BC said it planned to use the mostly wooded site for overflow parking and administrative offices.


The acquisition quickly drew scrutiny from Newton conservation groups, who feared what BC intended to do with the 17.4 acres of undeveloped forest at the back of the parcel—land sitting in the middle of Newton's largest contiguous woodland, flanked on all sides by public conservation areas.


In December 2019, following a multi-year campaign by the Friends of Webster Woods and the Newton Conservators, the Newton City Council voted unanimously to take 17 wooded acres from BC by eminent domain, paying the university $15.2 million in compensation.


BC called the sum "grossly underestimated” and filed suit. The legal battle—which has since led to questions about the validity of the original 1954 deed under which the state sold the land to the synagogue—remains unresolved, with a Middlesex Superior Court ruling in BC's favor now being contested by the city in Land Court.


BC has since renovated the former synagogue to hold an auditorium venue for BC’s Robsham Theater Arts Center and administrative office space.

Bellarmine House is a 24-bedroom mansion on 8.5 acres at the end of Howard Gleason Road in Cohasset, overlooking Cohasset Harbor with Minot’s Ledge Light lighthouse in the distance.


The house was built between 1880 and 1881 by architect H.H. Richardson for John Bryant and his wife Charlotte, the daughter of Frederick Law Olmsted, who designed the grounds.


The Jesuit Community of Boston College purchased the property in 1932 for $87,000. For decades, it served as a summer retreat for BC's Jesuits and a conference site for faculty.


In 2017, BC purchased Bellarmine House from the Jesuit Community and transferred it into the university's institutional portfolio. It now operates under BC's Division of Mission and Ministry, available to students, faculty, and staff for retreats and events.

Pine Manor College was a private university in Brookline that served a majority of first-generation and low-income students. When it experienced financial uncertainties due to COVID-19, BC agreed to acquire the college’s assets—including its 46-acre campus—as well as its liabilities, which totaled around $11 million.


The campus includes academic facilities, dorms, an auditorium, a library, a student activities center, six tennis courts, and a soccer field.


After the agreement was finalized, BC announced a $50 million endowment—which later grew to $100 million—to create the Pine Manor Institute for Student Success, a collection of programs supporting first-generation and low-income students.


At its center was Messina College, an associate’s degree program, which opened its doors in July 2024. Its inaugural class comprises 110 first-generation students, largely from Boston and other cities in Massachusetts.

When Mount Alvernia High School in Newton closed down in October 2023 due to enrollment and financial challenges, the Missionary Franciscan Sisters of the Immaculate Conception sold the property to BC in hopes of keeping it in the hands of a Catholic institution.


The 23-acre property, which consists of three buildings and a garage, is located across from Newton Campus and was purchased for $40.5 million.


This caused controversy among some Mount Alvernia alumni who opposed the closure. A petition signed by alumni states that the sale of the property is not a reflection of the values instilled by the school.


In December 2024, BC announced it would demolish two of the buildings and renovate 60,000 square feet of the 75,000-square-foot property.


The University is using the property to build a new School of Social Work building, an athletic facility for soccer and field hockey, and a space for the Urban Catholic Teacher Corps, a program housing teachers in the area.

As BC has acquired its larger properties, it has also steadily continued acquiring over 50 homes around its Chestnut Hill and Brighton campuses, most recently purchasing 9 Old Orchard Rd., possibly the oldest house in Newton, in 2024.


And as Leahy concludes his historic presidency, physical expansion continues.


The Catholic Religious Archives are set to finish this summer. The new social work building is being built. And just last week, BC announced a $125 million plan to renovate Carney Hall in 2028.


Rev. Jack Butler, S.J., will inherit these projects and Leahy's 430 acres when he takes office this July. A campus minister by background, Butler has said little publicly about physical expansion.


What he does with that inheritance—and whether the expansion continues into a new era—remains to be seen.


“I promise to devote myself to continuing BC’s upward trajectory,” he said upon being named president in February. “And making this great university an even stronger beacon of light and hope for the world.”
